Building Heating and Cooling Demand in the Changing Climate

My research is motivated based on the interaction between building sectors and climate change. Buildings accounting for more than one-third of global energy use and associated CO2 emissions are one of the largest contributor to global warming. A warmer climate will affect building energy consumption through changes in heating and cooling demands.

I use climate projections to evaluate the impact of climate change on building energy use over the Eastern United States. Currently, I am looking at the spatial and temporal changes of heating and cooling demand on state-level.


Research Highlights:

* By the end of the 21st century, the number of states where cooling is the annual dominant demand will increase from 4 to 18 among the 38 states in the eastern US. This change can affect the southern states in different aspects, including the amount of energy they use and the type of fuel they use. The increasing cooling demand will also increase the demand for electricity, and pose challenges on current utility and grid.

* The pattern of total energy demand is expected to be more homogenous. In the northern states, the reduction in heating demand outweights the increase in cooling demand, so total energy demand will decrease in the future. In the southern states, the increase in cooling demand is greater than the decrease in heating demand, which will lead to a net increase in total energy demand.
